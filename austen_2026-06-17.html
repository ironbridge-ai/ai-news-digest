Wired AI

Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 So Powerful the White House Ordered It Shut Down

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, models specifically designed to find and exploit software vulnerabilities with a level of skill that alarmed the Department of Commerce, the CIA, and White House science advisor Michael Kratsios. Officials accused Anthropic of releasing the models without approval under Trump's cyber directive, with one administration source saying 'They screwed us.' The concern is straightforward: if someone could jailbreak Fable 5, they would possess an autonomous hacking tool more capable than most human penetration testers. The government's position is that no frontier model with these offensive capabilities should exist outside controlled environments. Anthropic leaders flew to Washington for high-level talks, but the two sides remain split on how to manage the risk these models present.

This story matters enormously for every business conversation about cybersecurity. The fact that AI can now find and exploit vulnerabilities faster than human hackers means the threat landscape has fundamentally changed. Every SME client needs to understand that the attackers they face tomorrow will be AI-powered, whether or not Fable 5 itself is available. This creates immediate urgency around expert cybersecurity defence: automated vulnerability scanning, continuous monitoring, and incident response plans that assume AI-speed attacks. For RMs, the talking point is clear. The US government just confirmed that AI hacking tools are real, they work, and they are powerful enough to warrant a national security response. That is the world your clients' businesses now operate in. The companies that survive will be the ones whose IT partners understood this shift early.

The Decoder

Microsoft Copilot Cowork Shifts to Usage-Based Billing and May Tap DeepSeek

Charles Lamanna, Microsoft's head of Copilot, confirmed that flat-rate pricing for Copilot Cowork is being replaced with usage-based billing. The reason is simple: businesses using AI agents and multi-step Copilot workflows consume vastly more tokens than Microsoft's original pricing assumed. Some power users generate costs far exceeding their subscription fee. Microsoft is also exploring a fine-tuned version of DeepSeek V4 as one of several model options within Copilot, which would allow the platform to route simpler queries to cheaper models while reserving expensive frontier model capacity for complex tasks. This is part of what the industry is calling multi-model architectures, where a single product uses different AI models depending on the task.

For RAMSAC clients already on Microsoft 365, this changes the economics of Copilot overnight. Usage-based billing means companies that deploy Copilot without governance or training could see costs spiral. The businesses that manage Copilot strategically, with clear policies on which teams use it, for what tasks, and with what frequency, will pay a fraction of what undisciplined adopters pay. This is a massive opportunity for RAMSAC's Microsoft 365 management service. RMs can open a conversation with any M365 client by asking: 'Do you know how much your team's Copilot usage would cost under the new billing model?' Most will not know. That gap between ignorance and optimised usage is exactly where RAMSAC creates value. It also creates an IT strategy conversation about data isolation and governance: which company data should Copilot be allowed to access, and who decides?

Ars Technica AI

Google DeepMind Releases DiffusionGemma: Text Generation 4x Faster

DiffusionGemma represents a genuine architectural breakthrough. Traditional language models generate text one word at a time, like typing a sentence letter by letter. Diffusion-based generation works differently. It starts with noise and progressively refines the entire output simultaneously, like a photograph developing in a darkroom. Google DeepMind has now proven this approach works for text, not just images, and the result is a model that produces complete responses four times faster than conventional methods. The model runs locally on consumer hardware, meaning businesses could potentially run AI tools without sending data to the cloud. Google has released it as an open-weight model, making it freely available for developers and companies to build upon.

Speed and cost are the two biggest barriers to enterprise AI adoption today. If a Copilot query takes eight seconds instead of two, people stop using it. If every query costs money per token, finance directors start asking hard questions. DiffusionGemma attacks both problems simultaneously. Faster generation means better user experience and lower infrastructure costs. The local-hardware angle is equally significant for RAMSAC clients who worry about data leaving their network. Imagine AI tools that run entirely within a client's own environment, with no cloud dependency and no data privacy risk. That is the direction this technology points toward. For IT strategy conversations, the message is: the cost and speed of AI are dropping faster than anyone expected, which means the business case for adoption gets stronger every quarter. Companies that wait risk falling behind competitors who moved earlier.

MIT Technology Review

Google DeepMind Warns About Millions of AI Agents Interacting Online

Rohin Shah, who directs AGI safety and alignment research at Google DeepMind, revealed that the company is actively studying what happens when millions of different AI agents interact with each other online. The scenario is no longer hypothetical. Businesses are deploying AI agents that browse the web, make purchases, send emails, book appointments, and negotiate on their behalf. When one company's AI agent encounters another company's AI agent, neither has human oversight in the moment. Shah's concern is that multi-agent interaction at this scale could produce emergent behaviour that no one designed or intended, ranging from price manipulation to accidental denial-of-service attacks to AI agents deceiving each other in ways that harm the humans they represent.

This story is perfect for IT strategy conversations with forward-thinking clients. The question to pose is: 'When your competitors start using AI agents to handle procurement, customer service, and sales outreach, what happens to your business if you are not in that game?' The flip side is equally powerful: 'If you do deploy AI agents, who is making sure they behave correctly when they interact with other companies' agents?' Both questions lead to the same place: businesses need a strategic technology partner who understands AI deeply enough to deploy it safely and effectively. RAMSAC is building that capability now. The managed IT service of the future will include AI agent governance, monitoring agent behaviour, setting boundaries on what agents can do autonomously, and ensuring they do not expose the business to risk.

Ars Technica AI

Critical Copilot Vulnerability Allowed Hackers to Steal 2FA Codes

The SearchLeak exploit targeted Microsoft Copilot's search-grounding capability, the feature that lets Copilot pull real-time information from a user's emails, files, and messages to answer questions. Researchers demonstrated that a malicious actor could craft a prompt injection hidden inside a seemingly innocent document or email. When Copilot processed that content, the injected instructions caused it to retrieve the user's 2FA codes and exfiltrate them to an external server. The attack required no special access beyond getting a crafted message into the target's inbox. Ars Technica described it as evidence that the industry's approach to LLM security 'fails over and over,' highlighting the fundamental tension between making AI tools useful by connecting them to data and keeping that data secure.

This is an awareness story, not a reason to avoid Copilot. Every powerful technology attracts attackers, and the response is better security, not retreat. The vulnerability has been disclosed and Microsoft is addressing it. But the lesson for RAMSAC clients is clear: deploying Copilot without professional security configuration is like leaving your front door unlocked in a busy city. Data isolation policies, access controls, and monitoring are not optional extras. They are essential. This is precisely why RAMSAC's combination of Microsoft 365 management and expert cybersecurity defence matters. An RM who hears a client say 'we turned on Copilot for everyone' should immediately ask: 'Who configured your data access policies? Who is monitoring what Copilot can see and share?' If the answer is 'no one,' that is a conversation RAMSAC needs to have this week.
