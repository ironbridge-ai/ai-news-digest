Wired AI

Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 So Powerful the White House Ordered It Shut Down

The Trump administration forced Anthropic to pull its most advanced cybersecurity models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, citing national security concerns over their hacking capabilities. Dozens of cybersecurity experts protested the ban, arguing these frontier model systems are essential for defenders. This is the first time the US government has shut down a commercial AI model for being too capable.

  • Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, models designed to find and exploit software vulnerabilities with skill that alarmed the Department of Commerce, the CIA, and White House science advisor Michael Kratsios.
  • Officials accused Anthropic of releasing the models without approval under Trump's cyber directive, with one administration source saying 'They screwed us.'
  • The concern: a jailbroken Fable 5 would be an autonomous hacking tool more capable than most human penetration testers, and the government's position is that no frontier model with these offensive capabilities should exist outside controlled environments.
  • Anthropic leaders flew to Washington for high-level talks, but the two sides remain split on how to manage the risk.

The Decoder

Microsoft Copilot Cowork Shifts to Usage-Based Billing and May Tap DeepSeek

Microsoft is restructuring how businesses pay for Copilot Cowork, moving away from flat-rate pricing because token consumption has made it unsustainable. The company is also evaluating DeepSeek V4 as a cheaper model option running beneath Copilot. This signals a fundamental shift in how M365 AI tools will be sold and managed.

  • Charles Lamanna, Microsoft's head of Copilot, confirmed flat-rate pricing for Copilot Cowork is being replaced with usage-based billing.
  • Businesses using AI agents and multi-step Copilot workflows consume far more tokens than Microsoft's original pricing assumed, and some power users' costs now exceed their subscription fee.
  • Microsoft is also exploring a fine-tuned version of DeepSeek V4 as an option within Copilot, routing simpler queries to cheaper models and reserving expensive frontier model capacity for complex tasks.
  • This is part of what the industry calls multi-model architectures: a single product using different AI models depending on the task.

Ars Technica AI

Google DeepMind Releases DiffusionGemma: Text Generation 4x Faster

Google DeepMind has released DiffusionGemma, a model that uses diffusion-based generation to produce text four times faster than traditional approaches. This technique, previously used mainly for image creation, now applies to language. It could dramatically reduce the cost and waiting time for AI-powered business tools.

  • DiffusionGemma is a genuine architectural breakthrough. Traditional language models generate text one word at a time; Diffusion-based generation starts with noise and refines the entire output simultaneously, like a photograph developing in a darkroom.
  • Google DeepMind has proven this approach works for text, not just images, producing complete responses four times faster than conventional methods.
  • The model runs locally on consumer hardware, so businesses could run AI tools without sending data to the cloud.
  • Google has released it as an open-weight model, freely available for developers and companies to build on.

MIT Technology Review

Google DeepMind Warns About Millions of AI Agents Interacting Online

Google DeepMind is funding research into the dangers of mass multi-agent interaction, where millions of AI agents carrying out tasks for different people and companies collide online without human oversight. This is the first major lab to publicly flag this as an imminent safety challenge.

  • Rohin Shah, who directs AGI safety and alignment research at Google DeepMind, revealed the company is studying what happens when millions of different AI agents interact with each other online.
  • Businesses are already deploying AI agents that browse the web, make purchases, send emails, book appointments, and negotiate on their behalf, often with no human oversight in the moment.
  • Shah's concern is that multi-agent interaction at this scale could produce emergent behaviour nobody designed or intended, from price manipulation to accidental denial-of-service attacks to AI agents deceiving each other in ways that harm the humans they represent.

Ars Technica AI

Critical Copilot Vulnerability Allowed Hackers to Steal 2FA Codes

Security researchers disclosed a SearchLeak exploit in Microsoft Copilot that allowed attackers to extract two-factor authentication codes from users through a carefully crafted prompt injection attack. The vulnerability demonstrates that AI tools connected to sensitive data require rigorous security management.

  • The SearchLeak exploit targeted Microsoft Copilot's search-grounding capability, the feature that lets Copilot pull real-time information from a user's emails, files, and messages.
  • Researchers showed a malicious actor could hide a prompt injection inside an innocent-looking document or email. When Copilot processed it, the hidden instructions made it retrieve and exfiltrate the user's 2FA codes to an external server.
  • The attack required no special access beyond getting a crafted message into the target's inbox.
  • Ars Technica described it as evidence the industry's approach to LLM security 'fails over and over.'
