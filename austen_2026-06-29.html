Wired AI / TechCrunch AI

Fable 5 Returning, GPT-5.6 Restricted: Governments Are Now Gatekeeping the Most Powerful AI

This week delivered a remarkable double headline. Anthropic's frontier model Fable 5, which was forced offline after a jailbreak stripped its safety filters, is now close to being reinstated — but only after Pentagon and NSA sign-off, and initially only to over 100 pre-approved US companies and government agencies. At the same time, the White House asked OpenAI to delay the public release of GPT-5.6, a next-generation model with stronger capabilities in coding, science, and cybersecurity. OpenAI publicly pushed back, stating that government gatekeeping of AI models 'keeps the best tools from users, developers, enterprises, cyber defenders, and global partners who need them.' Two of the most powerful AI systems ever built are being managed like controlled substances. That is not a crisis — it is confirmation that these models have crossed a threshold that even governments cannot ignore.

For RAMSAC's clients, this story opens one of the most important conversations of the year. When governments intervene to restrict access to AI tools on national security grounds, it validates every serious business that is asking 'what is our AI strategy?' The models being restricted today will be in business hands tomorrow — and the companies that have prepared their IT infrastructure, their security posture, and their Microsoft 365 environment will be the ones who can deploy them safely and at speed. This is an IT strategy conversation waiting to happen. An RM can open with: 'The US government just put two AI models under controlled release because they were too capable to release freely. When those restrictions lift, your competitors will move fast. Are you in a position to move faster?' RAMSAC's role is to make sure the answer is yes.

Google DeepMind

Google's Gemini 3.5 Flash Can Now Use Your Computer — Not Just Answer Questions About It

Google DeepMind has introduced computer use in Gemini 3.5 Flash, a capability that allows an AI agent to see a screen, move a cursor, click buttons, type into fields, and complete tasks inside real software applications. Until now, AI tools answered questions or generated content. Computer use changes the category entirely: the AI is now an operator, not just an advisor. A user can instruct Gemini to open a spreadsheet, pull figures from a report, update a dashboard, and send a summary email — and the AI executes every step without human hand-holding. This is not a future concept. It is available now, and it connects directly to the tools RAMSAC's clients use every day across their Microsoft 365 environments.

The business implication is immediate and concrete. Any client who spends time on repetitive computer tasks — copying data between systems, formatting reports, filing documents, updating records — now has an AI agent that can do those tasks autonomously. For SMEs without large IT teams, this is transformational. It means one person can oversee work that previously required several. The managed IT conversation here is direct: deploying and governing AI agents that interact with live business systems requires careful configuration, access controls, and monitoring. That is exactly what a fully managed IT service delivers. An RM can ask: 'If an AI could sit at a computer and handle your team's repetitive admin tasks, which tasks would you hand over first?' Then follow with: 'We can help you deploy that safely.'

TechCrunch AI

A Cancer Patient Fed His Medical Data to Claude — and Got Insights His Doctors Hadn't Considered

When Connor Christou received a cancer diagnosis, he did what many founders do: he treated it as a systems problem. He fed every piece of data connected to his health — blood results, imaging data, wearable output, personal journal entries — into Anthropic's Claude. What he got back were patterns, questions, and connections that opened conversations with his medical team that might not have happened otherwise. The AI did not replace his doctors. It acted as a tireless analytical layer that synthesised more information, faster, than any single human could hold in their head. This story is not about medicine. It is about what happens when you give a powerful AI agent structured access to your most important data.

This is one of the most effective client conversation starters available right now, because it is human, real, and emotionally resonant. Every business leader has experienced a moment where important information existed inside their organisation but nobody could connect the dots in time. That is the problem AI solves at its best. For RAMSAC, this story unlocks a conversation about data readiness: before AI can deliver this kind of insight for a business, the data has to be structured, accessible, and secure. SharePoint, Teams, and the rest of the Microsoft 365 stack are where most SME business knowledge lives. An RM can ask: 'If an AI could read everything your business knows — every report, every email thread, every document — what question would you most want it to answer?' That question leads directly to a Copilot and IT strategy conversation.

OpenAI Blog / TechCrunch AI

OpenAI and Broadcom Build Jalapeño: A Custom Chip That Could End Big Tech's Nvidia Dependence

For years, the AI industry has run almost entirely on Nvidia's chips. That concentration of dependency is now being dismantled. OpenAI and Broadcom have co-designed Jalapeño, a chip purpose-built for LLM inference at scale — meaning it is optimised for the specific task of running large AI models quickly and efficiently, rather than being a general-purpose piece of silicon pressed into AI service. Google, Apple, and SpaceX are all building their own custom silicon too. The pattern is clear: every major AI company is investing in owning its own hardware layer. The goal is performance, cost reduction, and freedom from single-supplier risk — a concept RAMSAC's clients will recognise instantly as model diversification applied to infrastructure.

The downstream effect for businesses is lower AI costs and faster AI performance over the next two to three years. When the chips powering AI become more efficient, the price of running AI tools falls, and the capabilities available at a given price point improve. For RAMSAC clients considering whether now is the right time to commit to AI tooling, the trajectory of the infrastructure makes the case for acting: the tools available in 18 months will be more powerful and less expensive than today, but the organisations that will get the most from them are those that have already built AI-ready IT environments. This is an IT strategy story — and it gives an RM a concrete reason to say: 'The infrastructure behind AI is accelerating. Let's make sure your business is positioned to benefit.'

Ars Technica AI

Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of Running the Largest AI Model Cloning Attack Ever Recorded

Anthropic is demanding that Alibaba be punished after alleging the company systematically used 25,000 fake accounts to query Claude at massive scale. The purpose, Anthropic argues, was model cloning: extracting enough of Claude's behaviour, knowledge, and reasoning patterns through repeated interactions to replicate its capabilities without building them. This is not a traditional cyberattack. No system was broken into. No password was stolen. The attack vector was the AI model itself — and the weapon was volume. At 28.8 million exchanges, the alleged operation represents a new class of AI-specific threat that has no direct equivalent in conventional cybersecurity.

For RAMSAC, this story belongs in position 5 because it is primarily a legal and geopolitical dispute at the frontier model level, not a threat that directly affects an SME's day-to-day AI usage. But an RM who is asked about it should be ready with a clear answer. The lesson is not 'AI tools are unsafe.' The lesson is that the most capable AI systems are valuable enough to be targeted at nation-state scale — which reinforces why businesses need a trusted partner to manage their AI deployments, monitor for unusual usage patterns, and ensure their AI configurations are correctly governed. RAMSAC's cybersecurity and managed IT services provide exactly that layer of oversight. The AI era does not reduce the need for expert security guidance — it amplifies it.
